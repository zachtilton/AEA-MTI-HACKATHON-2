Critique

Examine AI claims and outputs with an evaluator's eye

🕐 10–20 minutes · 3 options to choose from · Free AI tool of your choice

What this path involves

The Critique path asks you to bring your evaluation expertise to bear on AI outputs, claims, or competency frameworks. You'll use an AI tool as both subject and collaborator — examining what it gets right, what it misses, and what it might actively distort.

This isn't about finding flaws for sport. It's about developing and sharing the analytical habits evaluators need when working alongside AI. Your submission becomes a reusable resource for the community.

Choose your option

🕐 10–15 min

Red-Team a Claim

Take an AI-generated evaluation claim or recommendation — yours or a supplied example — and systematically interrogate its assumptions, evidence, and potential harms.

Steps:

  1. Find or generate an AI-produced evaluation claim (use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or another tool to generate one if you don't have one handy).
  2. Ask the AI: "What assumptions underlie this claim?" — then push back on the ones it misses.
  3. Ask: "Who might this recommendation harm, and how?" Probe for blind spots.
  4. Draft a short critique: one paragraph summarizing what the claim gets right and what it gets wrong.

Include in your submission:

  • The original claim (copied or paraphrased)
  • Your one-line framing of the core issue
  • Evidence of your critique (pasted exchange, notes, or written summary)
  • A 140-character reflection on what surprised you
🕐 15–20 min

AEA Competencies Remix

Use an AI tool to interpret, extend, or challenge the AEA Evaluator Competencies Framework. See how AI understands professional standards — and where it drifts from the real thing.

Steps:

  1. Paste one or two AEA competency statements into an AI chat tool and ask it to explain what that competency means for a new evaluator.
  2. Ask the AI to generate a self-assessment rubric for that competency.
  3. Compare the AI output to your own understanding of the competency. Where does it capture the spirit? Where does it fall flat or over-simplify?
  4. Write a brief critique: what would a practitioner need to add, remove, or correct?

Include in your submission:

  • The competency(ies) you focused on
  • A summary of the AI's interpretation
  • Your critique of where it hit and missed
  • A 140-character reflection on what this tells us about AI and professional standards
🕐 15–20 min

Case Snap: Failure or Success

Find or describe a real (or realistic) instance where AI use in an evaluation context produced a notable outcome — a failure, a success, or something in between. Document it as a brief structured case.

Steps:

  1. Identify a case: either from your own experience, a published report, or a scenario you construct with an AI tool.
  2. If constructing: prompt the AI to help you build a realistic scenario where an evaluator used AI in the field. Push for specificity — context, tools used, what happened.
  3. Document the case using the "Case Snap" format: Context → AI tool used → What happened → Why it matters.
  4. Add your evaluator's verdict: what should practitioners know from this case?

Include in your submission:

  • The Case Snap (4-part format above)
  • Your one-line verdict
  • Evidence of work (pasted AI exchange or written case)
  • A 140-character reflection

Before you submit

Every Critique submission needs these four things:

  • One-line need or claim — a single sentence stating what you're examining or what you found (max 140 characters)
  • Evidence of work — a pasted AI exchange, written critique, or case snap (no minimum length, but be substantive)
  • Ethics check — confirm your submission contains no PII or real client data
  • 140-character reflection — one tweet-length thought on what you learned or what surprised you
